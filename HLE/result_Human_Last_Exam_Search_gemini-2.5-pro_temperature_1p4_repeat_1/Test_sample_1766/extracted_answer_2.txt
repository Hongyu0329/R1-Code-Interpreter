For a p-electron (l=1, s=1/2), the total angular momentum quantum number, j, can be 3/2 or 1/2.

**Case 1: j = 3/2**
*   The eigenvalue of $J^2$ is $j(j+1)ħ^2 = \frac{3}{2}(\frac{3}{2} + 1)ħ^2 = \frac{15}{4}ħ^2$.
*   The possible eigenvalues of $J_z$ are $m_jħ$, where $m_j = -\frac{3}{2}, -\frac{1}{2}, \frac{1}{2}, \frac{3}{2}$. The eigenvalues are therefore $-\frac{3}{2}ħ, -\frac{1}{2}ħ, \frac{1}{2}ħ, \frac{3}{2}ħ$.

**Case 2: j = 1/2**
*   The eigenvalue of $J^2$ is $j(j+1)ħ^2 = \frac{1}{2}(\frac{1}{2} + 1)ħ^2 = \frac{3}{4}ħ^2$.
*   The possible eigenvalues of $J_z$ are $m_jħ$, where $m_j = -\frac{1}{2}, \frac{1}{2}$. The eigenvalues are therefore $-\frac{1}{2}ħ, \frac{1}{2}ħ$.
